Crafting the Source-Locked Inquiry

A step-by-step guide to using NotebookLM for reading accountability that AI can't fake.

Why source-locked? NotebookLM is fundamentally different from ChatGPT or Gemini. It only references materials you upload — no outside training data, no hallucinated sources, no internet access. When a student asks it a question, the answer comes exclusively from your assigned readings. That's what makes it the reading accountability layer of an un-googlable assignment.

This guide covers the three tools featured in The Authenticity Lab and works with any LMS.

Create Your Notebook

Each assignment gets its own notebook. Think of it as a sealed container — only the sources you put in can be referenced.

  1. Go to notebooklm.google.com and sign in with your Google account
  2. Click "New Notebook"
  3. Name it clearly (e.g., "ENG 111 — Social Justice Readings — Fall 2026")
  4. Upload your assigned readings as sources — PDFs, Google Docs, web links, or YouTube videos all work
  5. Wait for NotebookLM to process each source (you'll see a checkmark when ready)

Pro tip: Upload 3–5 sources maximum per notebook. Too many sources dilute the specificity of student responses and make it harder to verify engagement.

Design Source-Locked Questions

This is where un-googlable happens. Your questions must require engagement with the specific content of your uploaded materials — not general knowledge about the topic.

  • Require specific evidence: "According to [Author], what are the three factors that..." forces citation from the actual reading
  • Ask for cross-source synthesis: "How does [Source A]'s argument about X contrast with [Source B]'s position?"
  • Demand analysis, not summary: "What assumption does [Author] make in paragraph 3 that a critic might challenge?"
  • Include page/section references: "In the section on [topic], the author makes a surprising claim. Identify it and explain why it matters."
English Composition

"How does Smith's use of personal narrative in Source 2 strengthen or weaken the statistical argument in Source 1?"

Psychology

"Source 3 cites a 2019 replication study. Does this support or challenge the framework presented in Source 1? Use specific data points."

History

"The primary source document contradicts the textbook account on two key points. Identify both and explain the significance."

Business

"Using only the case study data, build an argument for why the company's strategy failed. What does the financial data in Table 3 reveal that the narrative section omits?"

Avoid this: Generic questions like "What are the main themes of this article?" — these can be answered by any AI with a summary of the topic. Your questions should be impossible to answer without reading YOUR specific sources.

Test Your Questions Against the Notebook

Before assigning, run your own questions through the notebook. This is your stress test.

  1. Type each question into the NotebookLM chat
  2. Check that the AI cites specific passages from your sources (you'll see numbered citations)
  3. Verify the answers require genuine engagement — if the response is too generic, sharpen the question
  4. Try asking a question that isn't covered by your sources — NotebookLM should say it can't find relevant information. That's the source-lock working.
Test Prompt

Test prompt: "What does the research say about [topic NOT in your sources]?"

What you want to see: NotebookLM responds with "I don't have information about that in the provided sources." That confirms the source lock is working — your students can't get outside help from this tool.

Share the Notebook with Students

Students interact with the same notebook you built — same sources, same constraints.

  • Option A — Shared notebook (recommended): Click "Share" in the top-right corner and set to "Anyone with the link can view." Students open the notebook, ask questions, and interact with your sources directly.
  • Option B — Student-created notebooks: Provide students with the source files and have them create their own notebooks. This adds a setup step but gives you proof they uploaded the correct materials.

Workspace note: If your institution uses Google Workspace, sharing settings may be restricted to your domain. Test the share link from a non-institutional account or have students use personal Google accounts if needed.

Define the Submission Requirement

The notebook conversation IS the evidence. Students submit it alongside their final work.

  • The notebook share link — showing their conversation with the source-locked AI
  • A minimum interaction threshold — e.g., "at least 8 exchanges with the notebook demonstrating engagement with all assigned sources"
  • Source citation verification — students must confirm that NotebookLM's citations match the actual readings
Assignment Language

"Submit your NotebookLM conversation link showing a minimum of 8 substantive exchanges with the assigned readings. Your conversation should demonstrate that you engaged with all three sources and can identify connections between them."

Backup option: If sharing links isn't possible, students can export their conversation as a PDF (File → Print → Save as PDF). This always works regardless of platform restrictions.

Grade the Inquiry, Not Just the Answer

When reviewing notebook conversations, look for evidence of thinking — not just correct answers.

  • Question sophistication: Did the student ask follow-up questions that pushed deeper, or just accept the first answer?
  • Cross-source connections: Did they ask the AI to compare or contrast different readings?
  • Critical evaluation: Did they challenge an AI response or ask for clarification when something seemed incomplete?
  • Source coverage: Did they engage with all assigned materials, or just the easiest one?

Quick-Start Checklist

  • Create notebook at notebooklm.google.com with a clear, assignment-specific name
  • Upload 3–5 sources (PDFs, Docs, links, or YouTube) and wait for processing
  • Write source-locked questions that require specific evidence from your materials
  • Stress-test your questions by running them through the notebook yourself
  • Test the source lock — ask about something NOT in the sources and confirm it can't answer
  • Configure sharing and test the link from a different account
  • Write assignment instructions specifying the notebook link submission + minimum interactions
  • Set grading criteria that reward inquiry depth, not just correct answers
