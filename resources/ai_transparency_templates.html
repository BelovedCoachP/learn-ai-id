AI Transparency Statement Templates

Drop-in syllabus language for AI-integrated courses. Four versions — pick the one that fits your context and customize.

How to use these templates: Each template below is ready to copy and paste into your syllabus. They range from highly permissive (AI use welcomed with minimal requirements) to highly structured (specific documentation required for every AI interaction). Choose the version closest to your needs, then customize the bracketed sections. All templates include the core principle: AI use is welcome; hiding it is not.

Version A: Open Use

Permissive
Best for: Courses where AI is a routine tool — creative writing workshops, brainstorming-heavy courses, design thinking, or courses where the final product is heavily revised through human judgment.
Copy-Paste Syllabus Language

AI Tools Policy

You are welcome to use AI tools (such as ChatGPT, Gemini, NotebookLM, and others) throughout this course as thinking partners, research assistants, and drafting aids. AI is a professional tool, and learning to use it well is part of your education.

The one requirement: transparency. If AI contributed to your work in any meaningful way, note it briefly at the end of your submission. A simple statement like "I used ChatGPT to brainstorm initial ideas and refine my outline" is sufficient.

Using AI without disclosure is a violation of this course's academic integrity policy — not because AI use is wrong, but because hiding it is.

When to use this version: When your grading already emphasizes human judgment, revision, and originality — and the AI is genuinely a background tool rather than the primary content generator.

Version B: Documented Use

Moderate
Best for: Most courses — composition, social sciences, humanities, business. Works well when you want students to use AI but also want evidence of their thinking process.
Copy-Paste Syllabus Language

AI Tools Policy

Students are encouraged to use AI tools as part of their research, drafting, and revision process in this course. AI is a powerful tool for learning — when used with intention and transparency.

Documentation requirement: For assignments that involve AI, you must submit a shared conversation link (ChatGPT, Gemini, or NotebookLM) alongside your final work. This link shows your thinking process — how you prompted, what you questioned, where you revised — and is a graded component of your submission.

What counts as appropriate use: Using AI to brainstorm, generate outlines, challenge your arguments, check your logic, or explore alternative perspectives. What does not: Submitting AI-generated text as your own without revision, documentation, or critical engagement.

Using AI without documenting it constitutes a violation of this course's academic integrity policy.

Pair this with: The Process-Oriented Rubric. The shared conversation link becomes the evidence for the Prompt Quality and Fact-Checking criteria.

Version C: Full Process Documentation

Structured
Best for: Courses with high academic integrity stakes, upper-division seminars, capstone projects, or any course using The Authenticity Lab's full three-tool workflow.
Copy-Paste Syllabus Language

AI Tools Policy

This course integrates AI tools as a required component of the research and writing process. You will use AI deliberately and document your interactions as evidence of your learning.

Required submissions for AI-integrated assignments:

1. NotebookLM notebook link — showing your engagement with assigned course readings (minimum [8] substantive exchanges)

2. ChatGPT or Gemini conversation link — showing your iterative prompting process (minimum [4] rounds of refinement)

3. Final deliverable — your completed assignment, revised and authored by you

4. Process reflection — a [250]-word reflection on how AI influenced your thinking, what you changed, and what you learned

Assignments will be graded using a process-oriented rubric that evaluates prompt quality (25%), human synthesis (25%), source interrogation (20%), fact-checking (15%), and metacognitive reflection (15%).

Using AI without documenting it constitutes a violation of this course's academic integrity policy. Submitting AI conversation logs that show minimal engagement (e.g., a single prompt with no follow-up) will result in a reduced grade on the process criteria.

Customize the brackets: Adjust the minimum exchange counts and reflection word count to match your assignment complexity. For shorter assignments, 4–6 NotebookLM exchanges and a 150-word reflection may be sufficient.

Version D: Research & Citation Integrity

Research-Focused
Best for: Research methods courses, scientific writing, courses with heavy citation requirements, or any context where source verification is critical.
Copy-Paste Syllabus Language

AI Tools & Research Integrity Policy

AI tools may be used for brainstorming, outlining, and exploring research questions. However, all sources cited in your final work must be verified by you as real, accurately attributed publications.

AI hallucination warning: AI tools frequently generate plausible-sounding citations that do not exist. Any fabricated source included in your work — whether generated by AI or not — will be treated as an academic integrity violation. You are responsible for verifying every source.

Required for AI-assisted research assignments:

1. A shared AI conversation link showing your research process

2. A source verification log: for each AI-suggested source, confirm it exists by providing a DOI, URL, or library catalog entry

3. A brief statement identifying any AI-suggested sources you rejected and why

Using AI to locate sources is a legitimate research skill. Failing to verify those sources is not.

Why this version matters: Hallucinated citations are the most common and most consequential AI failure in academic work. This template makes source verification an explicit, graded skill rather than an assumed one.

Customizing Your Statement

  • Name specific tools: Listing "ChatGPT, Gemini, NotebookLM" by name removes ambiguity. Students need to know you understand the landscape — vague language like "AI tools" can feel uninformed.
  • State what's welcome, not just what's banned: Leading with permission ("You are encouraged to use...") reduces anxiety and increases compliance. Prohibition-first language drives AI use underground.
  • Define the documentation format: "Shared conversation link" is more specific and enforceable than "describe how you used AI." Links are verifiable; self-reports are not.
  • Connect to your integrity policy: Reference your institution's existing academic integrity policy by name. This gives the AI policy institutional weight without you having to create enforcement mechanisms from scratch.
  • Revisit each semester: AI tools change fast. Review your statement at the start of each term to ensure the tools you name still work the way you describe.

The line that changes everything: Regardless of which template you choose, the single most effective sentence you can add to your syllabus is: "Using AI without documenting it is the new plagiarism." It reframes the entire conversation from prohibition to transparency.

Institutional note: Before adding AI language to your syllabus, check whether your institution has an existing AI policy or is developing one. Your statement should complement — not contradict — institutional guidelines. If none exist, these templates can serve as a starting point for department-level conversations.
