Crafting the Process-Oriented Rubric

Grade the journey, not the destination. A complete rubric framework for AI-integrated assessment.

50% of the grade rewards HOW students think — not what they submit
"Don't ask 'Did they use AI?' — ask 'How did they use it, and how well?'"
The key move from AI-resistant to AI-integrated assessment

The Five Criteria

25%
Prompt Quality & Iteration
Did the student direct AI effectively and improve over rounds?
▼
Exemplary
Prompts are specific, strategic, and evolve across 4+ rounds. Student clearly directs the AI toward deeper analysis with each iteration.
Proficient
Prompts show intentional refinement across 3+ rounds. Student adjusts approach based on AI responses.
Developing
Some iteration present but prompts remain surface-level. Limited evidence of strategic refinement.
Beginning
Single prompt with no follow-up. Student accepts first AI output without challenging or refining.
25%
Human Synthesis & Original Thinking
Did the student add something AI couldn't generate on its own?
▼
Exemplary
Final work integrates AI-generated material with original analysis, personal insight, or novel connections. The human voice is unmistakable.
Proficient
Student adds meaningful original commentary and clearly transforms AI output rather than copying it.
Developing
Some original thinking present but final work relies heavily on AI-generated language and structure.
Beginning
Final submission is essentially unmodified AI output with no evidence of human synthesis or original thought.
20%
Source Interrogation
Did the student deeply engage with assigned sources through AI?
▼
Exemplary
NotebookLM conversation shows sophisticated inquiry across all assigned sources. Student asks analytical questions that connect and compare materials.
Proficient
Engagement with most assigned sources. Questions go beyond summary to analysis and comparison.
Developing
Engages with only 1–2 sources or asks primarily summary-level questions.
Beginning
No NotebookLM conversation submitted, or conversation shows minimal engagement with sources.
15%
Fact-Checking & Hallucination Detection
Did the student verify AI claims against actual sources?
▼
Exemplary
Student explicitly asks AI for source verification, identifies at least one hallucination or inaccuracy, and documents the correction process.
Proficient
Student verifies key claims and demonstrates awareness of hallucination risk in their reflection.
Developing
Some verification attempted but student doesn't catch obvious errors or fabricated citations.
Beginning
No evidence of fact-checking. Student accepts all AI output at face value.
15%
Reflection & Metacognition
Can the student articulate what they learned from the process?
▼
Exemplary
Reflection demonstrates deep metacognitive awareness — student articulates how AI shaped, challenged, or redirected their thinking with specific examples.
Proficient
Thoughtful reflection that connects the AI interaction to learning outcomes. Identifies specific moments of insight.
Developing
Reflection is generic ("AI was helpful") without specific examples or evidence of metacognitive processing.
Beginning
No reflection submitted, or reflection is a single sentence with no substantive analysis.

Why This Weighting Matters

Half the grade (50%) rewards how students think — prompting quality and original synthesis. AI cannot fake the iterative reasoning this rubric captures. A student who submits a polished final product but shows a shallow, single-prompt conversation will score lower than a student whose final work is rougher but whose process shows genuine intellectual engagement.

How to Implement This Rubric

Five steps from this page to your LMS.

1 Adapt the Weights to Your Context

These weights are a starting framework. Adjust based on your course goals:

  • Writing-heavy courses: Increase Human Synthesis to 30%, reduce Reflection to 10%
  • Research methods: Increase Fact-Checking to 20-25%
  • First-year courses: Increase Reflection to 20% to build metacognitive habits early
  • STEM problem-solving: Reframe Source Interrogation as "Data Interrogation" and adjust language accordingly

2 Define Your Submission Requirements

Students must submit three components for each assessed assignment:

  1. NotebookLM conversation link — evidence of source engagement (Source Interrogation criterion)
  2. ChatGPT or Gemini conversation link — evidence of iterative prompting (Prompt Quality & Fact-Checking criteria)
  3. Final deliverable + written reflection — the product plus metacognitive analysis (Human Synthesis & Reflection criteria)

3 Set Minimum Iteration Requirements

Specify a minimum number of AI interaction rounds before submission is accepted:

  • Minimum 4 rounds in ChatGPT/Gemini (aligns with the Socratic Loop: generate, challenge, steelman, verify)
  • Minimum 6-8 exchanges in NotebookLM (ensures engagement with all assigned sources)
  • Conversations below the minimum threshold receive an automatic "Beginning" rating on Prompt Quality

4 Add the Rubric to Your LMS

Translate the five criteria into your institution's rubric format:

  • Canvas: Create a rubric with 5 criteria and 4 rating levels. Use "Free Form Comments" for qualitative feedback on each conversation link.
  • Blackboard: Use the Rubric tool with percentage-weighted criteria matching the weights above.
  • Brightspace/D2L: Build as a custom rubric and attach to the assignment submission folder.
  • Any LMS: The criteria and levels translate directly into any standard rubric format.

5 Add Syllabus Language

Include this policy in your syllabus and assignment instructions:

"Students are encouraged to use AI tools as part of their research and drafting process. All AI interactions must be documented and submitted as part of the assignment. Using AI without documenting it constitutes a violation of the course's academic integrity policy. Assignments will be graded using a process-oriented rubric that evaluates prompt quality, source engagement, fact-checking, original synthesis, and metacognitive reflection."

✓

Key framing for students: "Using AI without documenting it is the new plagiarism." This single sentence resets expectations. It tells students that AI use is welcome — hiding it is not.

Grading Efficiently

This rubric is actually faster to grade than traditional essays. Here's how.

The 2-Minute Scan

Open the student's conversation links. Within 2 minutes you can tell:

  • Prompt Quality: Count the rounds. Read the first and last prompt. Did they evolve?
  • Source Interrogation: Scan NotebookLM — did they touch all sources? Any cross-referencing?
  • Fact-Checking: Look for the verification prompt. Did they ask for sources/DOIs?
  • Human Synthesis: Compare the conversation to the final submission. What did they change?
  • Reflection: Read the reflection. Does it reference specific moments from the conversation?
