Assignment Redesign Worksheet

Transform any existing assignment into a process-based, AI-integrated assessment in five steps.

How to use this worksheet: Pick one existing assignment you currently give students. Walk through each step below to redesign it so that the student-AI dialogue becomes a required, graded component of the submission. By the end, you'll have a draft assignment brief ready to pilot.

Identify the Original Assignment

Start with an assignment you suspect is vulnerable to AI-generated submissions. Describe it below as it currently exists.

Paste or summarize the instructions students currently receive.
What do students submit? (e.g., a paper, a slide deck, a discussion post)

Stress-Test with AI

Paste your current prompt into each tool below and record what happens. This reveals what AI can already do — and where students can hide behind AI output.

Quality? Accuracy? Did it meet your rubric as-is?
How did results differ from ChatGPT? Note any hallucinations or strengths.
Upload your course readings as sources. Could a student use it to bypass engaging with the material?
What's the core weakness? What can AI do that you assumed only students could?

Add the "Required Interaction" Layer

Now redesign the assignment so students must show how they used AI, not just what they produced. Choose one or more strategies below.

Require students to upload specific course readings into NotebookLM and submit a link to their notebook showing at least X questions they asked about the sources.
Require students to submit a shared ChatGPT or Gemini conversation link showing iterative refinement — prompting, fact-checking, and revising.
Require students to run the same query through multiple tools and write a reflection comparing the outputs.

Draft Your Redesigned Assignment Brief

Write the new instructions students will receive. Include what they submit, how they use AI, and how they'll be graded.

Write this as you would present it to students. Include the AI interaction requirement and all deliverables.
  • Final written product (essay, report, post, etc.)
  • AI conversation link(s) — ChatGPT, Gemini, or both
  • NotebookLM notebook link with uploaded course sources
  • Reflection paragraph on AI usage and human revision
  • Other:

Align to Learning Outcomes

Map your redesigned assignment back to your course outcomes. The AI interaction requirement should reinforce — not distract from — what you want students to learn.

Process-Oriented Rubric Template

Adapt this rubric to weight the learning process alongside the final product.

Criterion Exemplary (A) Proficient (B) Developing (C) Insufficient (D/F)
Prompt Quality & Iteration25% Conversation shows sophisticated, evolving prompts. Student challenges AI assumptions, narrows scope, and requests evidence. Clear progression from initial query to refined output. Multiple rounds of prompting with meaningful revisions. Student corrects at least one AI error or refines direction. Some iteration visible, but prompts are mostly surface-level rewording. Limited evidence of critical engagement. Single prompt with no revision. Conversation shows copy-paste behavior with no interrogation of outputs.
Source Interrogation20% NotebookLM log shows deep, analytical questions about course materials. Student synthesizes across multiple sources and identifies contradictions or gaps. Meaningful questions asked of course sources. Student connects AI-generated insights back to assigned readings. Surface-level questions (e.g., "summarize this"). Minimal connection between AI output and course content. No evidence of engaging with course sources through AI tools, or sources not uploaded.
Fact-Checking & Hallucination Detection15% Student identifies and documents specific AI errors or hallucinations. Provides corrections with cited evidence from course materials or credible external sources. Student notes at least one inaccuracy and provides a correction with a source. Student mentions AI might be wrong but provides no specific evidence or correction. No evidence of evaluating AI accuracy. AI output accepted at face value.
Human Synthesis & Original Thinking25% Final product clearly extends beyond AI output. Student's own argument, analysis, or voice is dominant. AI was a tool, not the author. Final product shows meaningful human revision. Student's perspective is present alongside AI-assisted content. Final product is lightly edited AI output. Student voice is minimal. Final product is indistinguishable from raw AI output. No evidence of human revision or original thought.
Reflection & Metacognition15% Thoughtful reflection on how AI shaped thinking. Student articulates what they learned from the AI interaction and what they would do differently. Adequate reflection addressing how AI was used and at least one insight gained from the process. Generic reflection (e.g., "AI was helpful"). No specific insight about the learning process. No reflection submitted, or reflection does not address AI interaction.
